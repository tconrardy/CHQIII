Photo by Tessa Conrardy.

“We’ve nearly doubled our per capita output of garbage since 1960, to the point where we now generate 50 percent more trash than Western Europeans and two to three times more than the Japanese.

These figures are only for municipal solid waste (MSW)—the waste we chuck from our homes, schools, and offices. It does not include agricultural waste, medical waste, construction debris, used tires, mining waste, and industrial waste. Taking all of this into account, each American is responsible for 35 tons of solid waste per year, or 2,700 tons over the course of his or her life. Imagine the weight of all the cars in a big rental-car lot at O’Hare or JFK airport. That’s the average American’s lifetime solid-waste footprint.”5

Cans on Trash Day.

Pittsburgh, PA, December 9, 2020.

Photo by Tessa Conrardy.

“Americans make more trash than anyone else on the planet, throwing away about 7.1 pounds per person per day, 365 days a year.

Across a lifetime this means, on average, we are each on track to generate 102 tons of trash.

Each of our bodies may occupy only one cemetery plot when we’re done with this world, but a single person’s 102-ton trash legacy will require the equivalent of 1,100 graves. Much of that refuse will outlast any grave marker, pharaoh’s pyramid or modern sky-scraper: One of the few relics of our civilization guaranteed to be recognizable twenty thousand years from now is the potato chip bag.”7

“Declaring 2020 the worst year ever is a form of collective commiseration that gives a name to a difficult experience and makes us feel less alone. It’s a coping mechanism… We’re choosing to believe that everything that is difficult will pass when the calendar changes.

It won’t, obviously. At 12:01 a.m. on January 1, 2021 people will still be living in poverty. Racism will still threaten the lives and livelihoods of Black Americans. Our health care system will still be inadequate, and climate change will still be coming for us. All of these things will continue to be propped up by choices we make on a daily basis, and by the choices of the people we elect.

The year is not the problem. We are. Which means we can do something about it.”8

This photo-essay was sparked by a photo of a discarded shopping magazine.**This photo is third-to-last in the photo-essay

The magazine had caught my eye as a telltale sign of the retail mania that ensues every year in the leadup to the holidays. I had already been thinking about the ways that the pandemic was shaping Black Friday, but this was the first time that I had stopped to think about how the pandemic was changing our waste.

I began scouting out trash to photograph, and thinking about how that waste was related to the pandemic. Some instances were obvious: a discarded mask, a dumpster full of chairs from a recently abandoned office building, a trashcan full of plastic bags in a usually tote-bag-toting neighborhood. Yet as I walked laps of my neighborhood on trash day, I found myself asking fewer questions about masks, disinfectants, and gloves, and more questions about seemingly mundane waste. Now that I was looking for garbage, it seemed impossible to ignore its sheer mass and omnipresence.

It wasn’t that there had never been litter in parking lots or trash discarded in shrubs before; I had simply been conditioned to ignore that waste. If we “go through our everyday lives with a highly developed practice of not-seeing,” then ignoring trash is the pinnacle of this practice.9 This sudden exposure to the garbage was also likely attributable to mass closures or partial closures of recycling and waste management facilities nationwide.10 My personal “not-seeing” practices and the infrustructures that promote such habits were crumbling simultaneously.

In searching for quotes, I encountered a similar trend. From my early observations about pandemic trash stemmed a hunch that PPE and packing waste would have skyrocketed since last year. Such waste has indeed increased significantly; between mask-wearing and increased individual packing, plastic usage has increased between 250% to 350% in 2020.11 In searching for quotes about trash more generally, however, I encountered research showing that the crisis of American waste is one that predates 2020, and worse, will postdate it as well.

The pandemic has not changed our waste trajectory; only accelerated it. In 2018, the World Bank predicted that global waste would increase by 70% by 2050.12 My further research also strengthened my commitment to the topic of waste, as I learned that waste dumpsites are the third-largest source of human-generated methane, and that the nation’s waste facilities and incinerators have historically been found in low-income communities of color; that is, when our waste is not outsourced to other countries.13,14,15 To contemplate issues of waste is to contemplate issues of climate change, racism, and imperialism.

I wanted to not only draw attention to the waste that we ignore, but to argue that the issue of American waste, like so many other issues that have come to a point in 2020, will not disappear with the pandemic. The pandemic has brought how we produce and handle waste to the forefront of the public consciousness, but “returning to normal” cannot mean re-turning a blind eye.

In crafting this argument, I decided that the introduction to my photo-essay should start on the same note that I began my research: within the specific scope of the pandemic. The first photo—a mask on wet asphalt—plays into exactly what one might expect from a photo-essay about pandemic waste. The quote that follows—a quotation of one of my co-workers, in reference to that mask—conveys the same attitude that I had going into this project: that recent excess of waste in our community now is unique to this moment, and that it will dissipate once that moment is over.

The subsequent photos and quotes reinforce that notion: that increasingly visible plastic bag waste is due to temporary bans on plastic bags, that discarded office furniture is a product of recent changes to corporate infrastructure, and that castaway vodka bottles can be attributed to recent spikes in alcohol consumption.

For this first, more pandemic-focused section of the photo-essay, I also chose to take fairly short-range photos, as these close crops force the reader to look at trash up close and personal in a way that emphasizes how unaccustomed we are to really looking at trash. Starting with a short-range approach was also narratively compelling, given the transition from a narrow, pandemic-specific lens in the introduction to a broader look at how this moment fits into trends of American waste. As the quotes transition from anecdotal mentions of pandemic trash to broader observations about American waste, so too do the photos adjust their scope, going from tight shots like that of the plastic bags to a middle-distance shot of the shopping magazine to further widened shots like the trashcans on the street.

I also chose to employ a black-and-white filter with color cutouts to help emphasize the trash in some of these later images. I wanted to emphasize the waste within scenes where we would normally ignore it, but I also wanted to test the reader’s eye. The last image is seemingly the least focused on the subject of trash, and has no such color cutouts. One might notice the hats, the Christmas decoration, or the garbage can before thinking to look for actual trash in the photo. The quote preceding this photo makes this fumble especially fitting; the last line reads, “One of the few relics of our civilization guaranteed to be recognizable twenty thousand years from now is the potato chip bag.” The very piece of trash that the reader might struggle to place is a Lays potato chip bag.

This final image also serves as an apt conclusion because it is such an ordinary household scene. How is my household contributing to this issue? How is my community contributing to this issue? How am I contributing to this issue? The subsequent quote prevents a potential misreading of that scene; I am not suggesting that individual waste is the core issue, but rather that we must be aware of whether we are advocating for positive change within the systems that we contribute to.
